# Mainrun: Reducing Validation Loss from 1.7545 to 1.0670

## TL;DR

Starting from a baseline per-character validation loss of **1.7545**, a sequence of targeted interventions brought the final per-character loss to **1.0670**, a **~39%** reduction. Two interventions accounted for ~87% of the gain: swapping SGD for AdamW with warmup (−0.370), and co-tuning batch size and learning rate (−0.270). Optimization, not capacity, was the dominant bottleneck — a model-size sweep across (n_layer, n_head, d_model) configurations was effectively flat.

## Approach

The first move was diagnostic, not corrective. The baseline was making slow but monotonic progress on validation loss — no overfitting U-curve, but plain SGD against a transformer, no warmup, no adaptive moments, and a still-falling curve at the end of training. Optimization headroom, not a broken model. Before changing anything, the priority was instrumenting the run: per-step gradient norms, per-group update-to-parameter ratios, RMS taps at three points in the residual stream, per-position validation loss, and per-token loss alongside per-character. Every metric was emitted once into structlog (the canonical store) and mirrored through wandb for visualization and transport off the GPU box. Every intervention was then framed as a pre-registered prediction: what the relevant telemetry should do if the change worked, and what it should do if it didn't. The discipline mattered more than any single prediction — articulating a mechanism before running compute sharpened decisions about what to bundle versus what to isolate, and twice in this campaign the readings pointed at non-obvious answers.

## What moved the needle

The single largest jump — about half the total reduction — came from replacing SGD with AdamW, adding 50-step linear warmup followed by cosine decay. SGD at the original learning rate was descending too slowly to use the available step budget. Three smaller corrections rode on top. Per-epoch headline reshuffle and per-batch uniform random window sampling fixed a fully-deterministic dataloader that was silently dropping the corpus tail (correctness; val-neutral). GPT-2-style scaled residual init — `0.02 / sqrt(2 * n_layer)` on attention and MLP output projections — was motivated by mid-stack RMS readings showing the residual stream overshooting to ~5.7 during warmup before settling. Retraining the BPE tokenizer on `train_titles` only removed val-set leakage from the vocabulary; this was *expected* to raise per-char loss numerically (val text could now route to `<unk>`) and instead dropped it slightly — within noise, but the leakage hadn't been helping in any measurable way.

The most consequential thought-process moment came from the LR sweep. The per-group update-to-parameter ratio was overshooting the ~3e-3 rule-of-thumb maximum during early training, which by textbook would call for *lowering* the learning rate. A sweep over lr ∈ {3e-4, 6e-4, 1e-3, 1.5e-3, 3e-3} found the opposite: raising lr to 1e-3 improved val loss, with stability acceptable on the metrics that actually mattered (no divergence, no gradient-norm explosion, no loss spikes). The 3e-3 rule of thumb is calibrated for long runs where small overshoots compound into divergence; at 7 epochs and ~1k steps, learning efficiency dominates that cosmetic ceiling. The overshoot is a budget, not a hard limit. That insight was load-bearing: removing dropout (which was hurting in the optimization-bound regime) gave a small further drop, and then co-tuning B=64 → B=256 with √-scaled lr 1e-3 → 2e-3 and warmup held at 50 absolute steps produced the second-largest jump of the cascade. The instinct to scale warmup proportionally with training length was wrong — warmup is a peak-LR property, not a fraction-of-training property.

The final cascade step was a tokenization upgrade: swapping the GPT-2 split regex applied inside `ByteLevel` for GPT-4's CL100K — finer segmentation of contractions, multi-digit numbers, and punctuation groups, giving BPE training cleaner semantic boundaries. The expected gain was modest, and modest is what landed, bringing per-char val loss to **1.0670**. Document-level attention masking was added separately during the cascade: headlines packed into a single context window are independent documents separated by `<eos>`, and the default causal mask lets attention cross those boundaries, exposing each token to noise from unrelated headlines. Replacing the causal mask with `same_doc & causal` confines attention to within-headline context.

## Negative findings

A handful of natural levers turned out not to help. **Vocab size:** sweeping {8k, 16k, 24k} under the train-only tokenizer left 16k at the bottom of a flat U-curve. **Capacity:** sweeping (n_layer, n_head, d_model) wider, narrower, deeper, and shallower produced no meaningful val differences despite massive overparameterization suggested by Chinchilla optimal ratios; the gains living entirely within noise here suggests the binding constraint at this scale is something else — optimization, or the intrinsic ceiling of headline structure — rather than parameter count. **Batch size beyond 256:** B=384 regressed cleanly; the gradient-quality gain from larger batches stops compensating for the reduced step count under the 7-epoch budget. **Dropout > 0:** already discussed; dropout=0 won. The negative findings ruled out the obvious "more parameters, more regularization, bigger vocab, bigger batches" instincts and freed cycles for the changes that actually mattered.

## Methodological notes

**Per-character vs per-token loss.** The frozen `evaluate()` reports per-character loss: cross-entropy summed over tokens, divided by the raw character count of the validation text. The fixed-in-raw-text denominator credits the whole pipeline (model + tokenizer) for predicting natural language — a more efficient tokenizer that compresses the same string into fewer well-predicted tokens correctly earns a lower per-char number. The discipline: per-char alone can't decompose a delta into "model" vs "tokenizer" improvement when both move at once. For tokenizer-changing runs (BPE train-only, pre-tokenization), per-token was tracked alongside, under each tokenizer respectively, to keep credit assignment explicit.

## Engineering scaffolding

The work that didn't directly move val loss but enabled everything that did:

- **Cloud iteration loop, backwards-compatible with the canonical container.** A vast.ai GPU box (~2-min full runs, ~40× faster than the canonical CPU container) for fast ablation. The cloud container and runtime code remain compatible with the canonical CPU container: `WANDB_API_KEY` is treated as optional, and its absence emits a `wandb_disabled` event so the assessment-side container still runs cleanly without credentials. `task fetch-run [run_id]` reverses the structlog/checkpoint flow back to local for analysis and submission.
- **Telemetry.** Per-step gradient norms, per-group update-to-parameter ratios via pre/post-step parameter snapshots, residual-stream RMS taps at three layers, per-position and per-token validation loss alongside per-character.
- **Logging architecture.** Structlog as the canonical store — every numeric metric emitted there once via a single `emit()` site. wandb is a derived view: visualization plus transport off the GPU box (logs and checkpoints are gitignored locally to avoid repo bloat).
- **Modular split.** The original `train.py` monolith was split into `config.py`, `model.py`, `data.py`, `optim.py`, `telemetry.py`, and a thinner `train.py`. Extraction-only — no semantic changes.

## What I'd do next

**Truncated cosine LR schedule.** Parameterize the cosine decay as if for a longer run and terminate at the 7-epoch mark, exiting at a moderate LR rather than completing the decay tail. Cosine spends disproportionate time at its extremes (slowest at start and end), and in a fixed-budget regime the slow-decay tail may be wasted steps that could have been spent at a more productive mid-LR. Distinct from raising `min_lr_frac`, which keeps the cosine shape but floors it earlier — truncation skips the slow tail entirely. Risk: noisier final-step weights from skipping the implicit cool-down "polish" of a completed cosine.

**Modern architecture and optimizer variants.** The cascade ran end-to-end on a vanilla GPT-2 stack: LayerNorm, GELU MLP, learned absolute positional embeddings, plain AdamW. The capacity sweep tested *size* but never *kind*. Worth investigating in this regime, without strong priors for how any of these behave at this scale and step budget: Sophia, Lion, Muon, or schedule-free optimizers; RoPE; SwiGLU MLPs; RMSNorm.
